# Funduq al-Najjarin: A Moroccan Funduq in the Fez Medina

## Introduction

Funduq al-Najjarin sits in the carpenters’ quarter of the Fez medina (al-najjarin means “the carpenters”). It was built in 1711 under the Alawi sultan Moulay Isma’il, and the work it did was the work funduqs had been doing in Moroccan cities for centuries. Traveling merchants lodged there, stored their goods on the lower floors, and did business alongside the resident artisans of the quarter.[^1] This is a very different kind of building from the fortified caravanserais of rural Khurasan or the port khans of Acre. Instead of sitting on a long-distance route, it is wedged into a dense urban medina, and the architecture follows from that. The footprint is compact — three stories around a small courtyard — and it displays the full range of Moroccan decorative styles of carved cedar, sculpted stucco, and zellij tilework.

[^1]:Archnet entry for Funduq al-Najjarin, https://www.archnet.org/sites/1397.

## The Moroccan Funduq as a Building Type

Fez was already one of the major funduq cities of the Islamic world by the twelfth century. Olivia Remie Constable notes that during the reign of the Almohad caliph Muhammad b. Ya’qub (1199–1213), “467 funduqs were reportedly assessed for taxes in Fez,” which is an incredible number of commercial hostelries for a single medieval city, even along the Silk Road.[^2] These buildings worked together as a kind of distributed urban infrastructure. They held goods, generated tax revenue, and gave traveling merchants a way to plug into the daily life of the medina. Many were also tied into faith-based funding, (endownments or waqfs) and made money for religious institutions: the twelfth-century Qarawiyyin mosque, for example, drew “substantial revenues from a commercial funduq in the city.”[^3]

[^2]:Olivia Remie Constable, *Housing the Stranger in the Mediterranean World: Lodging, Trade, and Travel in Late Antiquity and the Middle Ages* (Cambridge University Press, 2003), 79.

[^3]:Constable, *Housing the Stranger*, 78.

By the early eighteenth century, when Funduq al-Najjarin was built, Moroccan funduqs had developed a style that was recognizable as their own — not like either the Levantine khan, not the Mamluk wikala. The basic plan stayed small and consistent: three storeys around a central courtyard, ground-floor space for animals and goods, and upper-floor rooms rented out to merchants and artisans. The decoration is what set them apart most visibly. Where Seljuq Iran tended to work patterns in brick and Ottoman Acre relied on granite columns, Moroccan craftsmen worked in three other media. Cedar was carved into galleries, balustrades, and ceilings. Plaster — locally called gebs — was sculpted into arches, spandrels, and wall panels. Zellij, the hand-cut geometric tilework Morocco is best known for, went down across dados and floors.  This led to a veritable riot of visual textures - wood, tile, plaster, all shaped for beauty.

## Architecture and Plan

Funduq al-Najjarin is a brilliant example of all of this. The central courtyard is square and relatively small, with three storeys of arcaded galleries rising above a tiled floor and a central fountain. Square masonry pillars carry the ground-floor open stalls. The upper galleries project out over the courtyard on carved cedar cornices and are closed in by rails (balustrades) in the same wood. White carved stucco fills the every arch and runs in delicate bands above the galleries, and zellij dados of many colors wrap around the base of the courtyard walls. A skylight now covers what was originally an open courtyard — a later addition that protects the interior from the weather, sun, sand, and time.

The carpentry is what most people come to see, and it is the reason the building now houses the Nejjarine Museum of Wooden Arts and Crafts. The geometric patterns worked into the galleries, balustrades, doors, and ceilings come out of a long Moroccan woodworking tradition, and the fact that they have survived where they were originally placed — rather than being removed, broken up and absorbed into museum collections elsewhere — makes this one of the most intact early-modern Maghrebi interiors still in situ. The craftsmanship is not surprising for a building put up in the carpenters’ quarter, but it is a remarkable reminder about how much money the Alawi sultans were willing to spend on the medina’s civic and religious architecture during this period.

## Building History and Adaptive Reuse

The building operated within its original commercial purpose for nearly three centuries before falling into disrepair in the twentieth century. A major restoration in the 1990s, paid for by the Karim Lamrani Foundation, brought it back into public use — not as a working funduq but as a museum of Moroccan wooden arts. It opened as the Nejjarine Museum of Wooden Arts and Crafts in 1998.[^4] Most of what has been written about the building emphasizes how thoroughly the original architecture has been preserved. The three-storey galleries, the carved cedar balustrades, the stucco and zellij decoration, and the central courtyard are all still there, and the museum’s displays — Moroccan woodworking tools, musical instruments, household objects — are arranged around the perimeter of the courtyard.

[^4]:Archnet entry for Funduq al-Najjarin, https://www.archnet.org/sites/1397; Nejjarine Museum of Wooden Arts and Crafts, official website.

## Significance

Funduq al-Najjarin is one of the most complete surviving examples of Moroccan funduq style, and one of the few buildings of this kind to have been kept up through institutional reuse and restoration rather than aging or being abandoned. It manages to display the full range of the early-modern Moroccan traditional architectural decorative elements into a small footprint, and its placement in the carpenters’ quarter shows how funduqs of this type were tied into specific artisan neighborhoods of the medina rather than set apart at the urban edge, urban rather than rural or urban at the border. Seen next to Ribat-i Sharaf and Khan al-Umdan, it shows a third way of building an Islamic hostelry. Ribat-i Sharaf is a fortified monument standing by itself on a Khurasani road, a rural caravanserai. Khan al-Umdan is built for harbor-scale Mediterranean trade. Funduq al-Najjarin is smaller and more ornamented than either, and it was built directly into the working life of an inland artisan quarter.
