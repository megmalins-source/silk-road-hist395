# Khan al-Umdan: An Ottoman Port Khan in Acre

## Introduction

Khan al-Umdan sits in the southeast corner of Acre’s old city, right next to the port. It is the largest and best-preserved Ottoman khan in the region. Unlike the rural caravanserais of Khurasan (our example is Ribat-i Sharaf), which look like walled fortresses, Khan al-Umdan was built as an urban building, which necessitated different architecture to perform as it was intended to. Its job was not to give shelter to long-distance caravans crossing empty country from one hostel to the next, but to support the daily commercial life of a busy Mediterranean port. Two floors of arcaded galleries surround a big rectangular courtyard, sitting on the foundations of an older Crusader-era customs building.  This is a much later moment in Islamic hostelry architecture than Ribat-i Sharaf, and a very different one — it was shaped by Ottoman port cities, not by the Silk Road caravan trade.  Boats and ships carried goods in addition to beasts of burden and that difference is shown in the design.

[^1]:Andrew Petersen, *A Gazetteer of Buildings in Muslim Palestine, Part 1*, British Academy Monographs in Archaeology 12 (Oxford University Press, 2001), 87.

## Architecture and Plan

Khan al-Umdan is a two-story structure arranged around a rectangular courtyard surrounding two tiers of arcades — the lower arcade resting on red and black granite columns, from which the building takes its name (*al-umdan* meaning "the pillars").[^2] Petersen describes three entrances: the main gate on the north side, with two additional gateways on the south. Above the main north gate is a square clock tower built at the turn of the twentieth century to commemorate the silver jubilee of Sultan Abdul Hamid II, a later addition that gives the building its most distinctive silhouette against the Acre skyline.[^3]

[^2]:Petersen, *Gazetteer*, 87.

In 1943, the British archaeologist R. W. Hamilton inspected the khan and recorded its condition. His observations, preserved in the Palestine Archaeological Museum archives, describe the 'colonnaded gallery' behind the arcade as containing "a range of big barrel-vaults with high windows in the back wall," with masonry-vaulted stairs leading from the courtyard to the upper floor. The rooms on the upper gallery are small barrel-vaults, and at the southeast corner an L-shaped passage leads to latrines built into the sea wall—a reminder that the building's eastern side opens directly onto the Mediterranean.[^4]

[^4]:Petersen, *Gazetteer*, 87, citing R. W. Hamilton's 1943 field notes (PAM Hamilton 9.11.43).

## Urban Context and Function

As an urban khan in a Mediterranean port city, Khan al-Umdan served a different kind of business model than the isolated rural caravanserais of Iran. Constable's analysis of medieval Muslim hostelry architecture emphasizes that in the Levant and Syria, the word *khan* had become the dominant term for urban commercial lodging, distinct from the Mediterranean *funduq* further west and the rural Persian *caravanserai* further east.[^5] The khans of the Levantine coast, unlike their Iranian counterparts, were not fortresses sprawling on empty landscapes but were integrated commercial buildings existing deeply within the daily fabric of port cities — offering storage space for incoming and outgoing cargo, lodging for visiting merchants, and sometimes locked facilities used by urban authorities for short-term confinement of travelers.  All at a price, but secure - a tradeoff that seemed to work for all.

[^5]:Olivia Remie Constable, *Housing the Stranger in the Mediterranean World: Lodging, Trade, and Travel in Late Antiquity and the Middle Ages* (Cambridge University Press, 2003), 59–61.

Khan al-Umdan's harbor location made it well suited to the movement of goods across the harbor. Ships arriving at Acre's harbor could unload cargo directly into the ground-floor vaults of the khan; merchants could stay on the upper floor while conducting negotiations or waiting for their goods to be weighed, taxed, and released. This integration of storage, lodging, and commercial transaction within a single building was, as Constable observes, the defining feature of the medieval Islamic commercial hostelry—not innovated at Acre, but an institutional pattern with origins stretching back to the earliest Islamic *funduq*.[^6]

[^6]:Constable, *Housing the Stranger*, 40–42, 88–90.

## Building History

Khan al-Umdan has a rich history going back to the Crusader period. The archaeologist Denys Pringle's work on Acre identifies the foundations beneath Khan al-Umdan as those of the Royal Customs House of the Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem—a building that would have served very much the same commercial function in its time.[^7] The Ottoman khan that now stands was built in the late eighteenth century, generally attributed to the reign of Ahmed Pasha al-Jazzar, the Ottoman governor of Sidon (1775–1804), who did extensive rebuilding of Acre after its capture by the Ottomans.[^8] The long stretch of commercial activity through nearly six centuries demonstrates how central the port economy remained to Acre's identity across multiple political regimes. A single building served as Crusader customs house, Ottoman khan, and now, a modern heritage site.

[^7]:Petersen, *Gazetteer*, 87, citing Denys Pringle, *Secular Buildings in the Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem: An Archaeological Gazetteer* (Cambridge University Press, 1997), 15–17.

## Significance

Khan al-Umdan is a valuable example of Ottoman commercial architecture in the eastern Mediterranean and a very important surviving piece of Acre's late Ottoman port economy. Its two-tier arcade, granite columns, and integrated water supply through the courtyard fountain (*sabil*) mark it as an urban khan of impressive size and architectural ambition, despite functioning as a working commercial facility rather than a royal monument or palace. Compared to the isolated fortress of Ribat-i Sharaf, Khan al-Umdan illustrates how the same architectural features — courtyard, arcade, central water source, gated enclosure — could be adapted to radically different environments across the Islamic world.
